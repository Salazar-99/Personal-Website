What I'm Reading

I like having multiple books to read at the same time, it keeps things fresh. Below are the books I am currently reading, my motivation for reading them, and some thoughts about them.

The Annotated Turing

by Charles Petzold

Does there exist an algorithm which takes as input a mathematical statement and returns as output whether or not the statement is true in any structure satisfying a set of predefined axioms? This is David Hilbert's famous Entscheidungsproblem, which he posed in 1928. In 1936, Alan Turing published his seminal paper "On Computable Numbers, With An Application To The Entscheidungsproblem." In it, he describes the construction of mathematical objects now known as Turing Machines and through their application proves that there exists no solution to the Entscheidungsproblem in the general case. His work on Turing Machines formed the theoretical foundations underlying all of modern computing. This book steps through the original paper offering helpful explanations, clarifications, and examples along the way. Although I previously had a cursory understanding of the Turing model of computation from a course in Algorithms and Complexity, I am finding that stepping through the constructive proof via the words of Turing himself is providing me with a much deeper understanding. Paraphrasing the author, perhaps the most interesting aspect of the paper is that before anybody really knew what digital computers could do, Turing laid out what they couldn't do.

The Art of Doing Science and Engineering: Learning to Learn

by Richard W. Hamming

Throughout my education I was often bothered by questions like: "How did Einstein get to the idea of spacetime curvature and the Field Equations?", "How did mathematicians realize that this definition of a Group would yield such a rich theory?", and "How did Hilbert know this was the right way to formulate Quantum Mechanics?" Sometimes I found satisfying answers and sometimes I did not. When we learn about technical things we most often get the polished version resultant of decades or centuries of refinement and hindsight but rarely were the original ideas so well organized. In this book Richard Hamming describes the thought processes behind some of the biggest technical achievements in electrical and computer engineering of the last century. He posits that one can train themselves to be an effective thinker and uses the examples in the book to illustrate some of the ways one might achieve that. Thinking about the way that you think, often referred to as metacognition, is a topic of great interest to me and this is one of the only books I've stumbled upon that treats it in a technical context. The notion that you can train yourself to have more great ideas is a fascinating one.

The Elements of Computing Systems: Building A Modern Computer From First Principles

by Noam Nisan, Shimon Schocken

When the famed physicist Richard Feynman passed away in 1988 he left two statements on his blackboard "What I cannot create I do not understand" and "Know how to solve every problem that has been solved." I came across an image of that final blackboard early on in my Physics studies and it has stuck with me ever since. This book walks you through the construction of a rudimentary modern computer starting with boolean alegbra and logic gates all the way up to building an operating system and high level programming language on top of that. It comes with software tools and projects to complete along the way. I'm really enjoying learning how to create a computer from first principles and thus really understanding one of the most important tools in our modern world.

Unix and Linux System Administration Handbook

by Garth Snyder, Evi Nemeth, Trent Hein, Ben Whaley

I have been using Linux (more specifically the latest Ubuntu distribution) as my main operating system for a little over a year now. This book is a reference manual for all things Unix and Linux. While I can see it coming in handy in my career I'm mostly reading select chapters to gain a better understanding of what's going on under the hood of the systems I use every day.
